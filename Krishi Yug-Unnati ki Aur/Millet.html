Millet

Millet- Millets are a group of highly variable small-seeded grasses, widely grown around the world as cereal crops or cereals for forage and human food. Millet is an important crop grown in a variety of soils. It provides tasty and nutritious fodder.

It is one of the important crops of Southeast Asia, China, India, Pakistan, Arabia, Sudan, Russia and Nigeria. In India, it is grown in Haryana, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh. Millet may have been consumed by humans for about 7,000 years and likely had an important role in the growth of multi-crop agriculture and settled agricultural societies.
Millets are a group of highly variable small-seeded grasses, widely grown around the world as cereal crops or grains for fodder and human food. Most species generally referred to as millets belong to the tribe Paniceae, but some millets also belong to various other taxa.
Millets are important crops in the semiarid tropics of Asia and Africa (especially in India, Mali, Nigeria, and Niger), with 97% of millet production in developing countries.The crop is favored due to its productivity and short growing season under dry, high-temperature conditions.
The various species called millet were initially domesticated in different parts of the world most notably East Asia, South Asia, West Africa, and East Africa. However, the domesticated varieties have often spread well beyond their initial area. Specialized archaeologists called palaeoethnobotanists, relying on data such as the relative abundance of charred grains found in archaeological sites, hypothesize that the cultivation of millets was of greater prevalence in prehistory than rice, especially in northern China and Korea. Millets also formed important parts of the prehistoric diet in Indian, Chinese Neolithic and Korean Mumun societies.

Sowing Method:-
Sowing of millet crop is 25 cm. in rows and 1.5-2 cm from the seed drill. Must be done at a distance. For this 8-10 kg. Seed per hectare is sufficient. Seed should be treated with Agrosan GN or Thiram (3 g/kg seed) before sowing.

Land preparation: Light or loamy loamy soil is suitable for millet. The drainage of the land should be good. The field should be prepared by first ploughing with soil turning plow and other 2-3 ploughings by indigenous plow or cultivator.

Time of sowing :- March to mid-April is the best time for summer sowing in irrigated areas. First fortnight of July is suitable for Kharif crop. In South India, sowing is done during the Rabi season from October to November.

Temperature :- The millet plant needs a temperature of 25 C to germinate. Whereas 30 to 35 degree Celsius temperature is needed to grow up. But its plant can give good yield even in 40 degree Celsius temperature.

Crop circle :- Proper crop rotation is essential to get higher yields from the millet crop. In unirrigated areas, next year after millet, pulse crops like guar, mung, moth should be taken. In irrigated areas, bajra-mustard, bajra-cumin, bajra-wheat crop rotation should be used.

Fertilizers and Chemical Fertilizers:- Crop plants require nutrients for proper growth. Therefore, while preparing the land, 5 tonnes of well decomposed cow dung or compost should be used for millet crop. After this, 40 kg in the rain based crop of millet. nitrogen and 40 kg. Phosphorus is required per hectare. Keep in mind that fertilizers should be used only on the basis of soil test.

Harmful pests and diseases and their prevention:- Termites cause damage by eating the roots of millet plant, for its prevention, while preparing the field, quinalphos or chlorpyrifos 1.5 percent powder should be mixed in the ground at the rate of 25-30 kg/ha. In addition, the seeds were mixed with Chlorpyriphos 4 ml. Seed treatment should be done at the rate of /kg seed.
Katra - Bajra crop is damaged by cutting its braid at the initial stage, for its prevention, quinalphos 1.5 percent powder should be sprinkled at the rate of 20-25 kg/ha.
White Braid - For the control of white braid in millet, sow 3 kg carbofuran 3% or quinolphos 5% particles with 15 kg DAP in one kg seed.20
Root bug - For the control of root bug in millet, apply quinolphos 1.5 percent or methyl parathion 2 percent powder at the rate of 25 kg per hectare.
Jogia - For the control of Jogia, after 21 sowing, spray Mancozeb 1 kg/acre.
Argat or Chepa - For its prevention, seed should be sown by treating with Thiram 75% WS @ 2.5 grams and Carbendazim 50% WP @ 2.0 grams per kg seed. Spraying of Carbendazim + Mancozeb with 40 g/15 liter of water should be done when symptoms of the disease are observed.
Smut - For the prevention of smut, seed should be sown after treating the seed with Thiram 75% WS @ 2.5 g and Carbendazim 50% WP @ 2.0 g per kg seed. And on showing symptoms of disease, 40 g/15 mixed chemical of Metalaxyl + Mancozeb should be given. Spray it by mixing it in a liter of water.

Weed control :- Weeding should be done with weeder less culture at the stage of 25-30 days. Spraying of Atrazine (0.5-0.75 kg/ha, 600 liters of water) before harvesting is effective for the crop, but millet cowpea, 1 kg. Elaclor should be used before sowing.

Irrigation:- Millets are generally rainfed crops. Moisture is the most important place for proper growth of millet plants. For irrigated areas, when sufficient moisture is not received by rain, irrigation should be done from time to time. 3 - 4 irrigations are sufficient for the crop of Bajra. Keep in mind that there should be moisture in the field at the time of grain formation. Due to this, the development of grain is good and the yield of grain and fodder increases.

Harvesting :- In harvested species, harvest at 69-75 days after sowing (50% complete stage). In multi-cut species, first harvesting is done at 40-45 days and then after 30 days interval. Thus, 450-950 quintals of fodder is obtained from scientifically grown crops.

Storage :- Bajra cultivation increases the production of millet to about 25 to 30 quintals per hectare. Whereas up to 70 quintals of dry fodder is available. For storing grain, sacks should be kept in a moisture free place.
