Ashwagandha

Ashwagandha- herb or medicine is very important and ancient whose roots have been used as medicines in Indian traditional system of medicine such as Ayurveda and Unani. The leaves of Ashwagandha are light green, oval, generally 10 to 12 cm long. Generally its flowers are small, green and hourglass shaped. Usually the ripe fruit is orange and red in color.
In Ashwagandha farming, one can earn well by making farming management and suitable market model. The root and leaf seeds of the Ashwagandha plant are used. Ashwagandha crop as you know, is a Rabi crop, and the main Ashwagandha growing states in India are Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Gujarat, Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh etc.
Local Names of Ashwagandha in India: Ashwagandha, Asgandha, Ajagandha, Nagori Asgandha, Rasabhari (Hindi), Amukkara, Amukira, Asurgandi (Tamil), Amukuram, Tritavu, Aymodakam (Malayalam), Dhuppa (Bengali), Keramdingaddi, Kanuzuki, Kanuki, Kanuki Tilli (Marathi), Asod, Ghoda Ahan, Ghoda Aukan, Asun, Assam, Ghodsoda (Gujarati), Penneru, Vajigandha (Telugu).

Ashwagandha is an important herb in Ayurvedic medicine. This is one of the world's oldest medical systems and one of India's healthcare systems. In Ayurvedic medicine, ashwagandha is considered a Rasayana. This means that it helps maintain youth, both mentally and physically. There is some evidence to suggest that the herb can have neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory effects. Inflammation underpins many health conditions, and reducing inflammation can protect the body against a variety of conditions.


Ashwagandha Varieties Jawahar which is small in stature and is most useful for high density planting. The variety yields in 6 months with a total withanoide content of 0.30 percent in dry roots.

Sowing time For the cultivation of Ashwagandha, nursery should be established in the month of June and July. Seeds should be planted before the onset of monsoon.

Distance Line to line distance should be kept 20 to 25 cm and plant to plant distance should be 8 to 10 cm.

Seed depth To sow the seeds 1-3 cm. Sow deep

Printing Method Method of Transplanting Generally broadcast or spraying method is suitable.

Seed quantity Seed Quantity 12 kg of seed is required per hectare.

Seed treatment Transplanting is prone to seed borne diseases, so seed should be treated before sowing in nursery bed or field. Seeds should be treated with Thiram @ 3 g/kg of seed.

Earth Ashwagandha crop can be well drained in sandy loam or light red soil with drainage between 7.5 to 8.0.

Farm Preparation The soil of the field selected for cultivation of Ashwagandha is well pulverized by plowing or spinning. Two to three ploughings should be done to bring the soil to fine state and this should be done before the rainy season. Supplementation with well decomposed field manure (FMY) is beneficial to get good crop yield. Ashwagandha is generally grown in areas that are not well covered by irrigation systems.

Required Climate Ashwagandha crop can be grown up to an altitude of 1500 meters above sea level. The semi-tropical regions which receive about 500 to 800 mm of annual rainfall are best suited for its cultivation. This crop requires dry weather during its growing period and the temperature between 20C to 38C is best suited for cultivation. This crop can tolerate temperature up to 10C.

Fertilizers and Chemical Fertilizers Ashwagandha crop responds very well to farm yard manure (FYM), vermicompost and green manure. Generally, this crop demands 10 to 12 tonnes of well-rotted manure or 1 to 1.5 tonnes of vermicompost per hectare. If the soil has average fertility, supplementing with 15 kg of nitrogen and 15 kg of potassium per hectare gives a higher yield.In case of poor fertility soils, 40 kg nitrogen and 40 kg potassium per hectare is recommended for higher root yield.

Weed control For weed control, first weeding should be done within 21 to 25 days of sowing and second weeding should be done 21 to 25 days after first weeding.

Irrigation Ashwagandha crop does not tolerate excessive irrigation or water logging conditions. Light irrigation at the time of transplanting ensures better establishment of seedlings in the soil. Irrigate the crop once at an interval of 8 to 10 days for better root yield.

Harvest time Dry leaves and reddish-orange plums indicate maturity. Ashwagandha crop is ready in 160 to 180 days after transplanting. The entire plant is removed along with the root, after which the upper part is separated by cutting off the top part of the stem. After that it is cut into small pieces of 8 to 10 cm and put to dry.

Ashwagandha yield Crop yield depends on several factors such as soil fertility, irrigation and farm management practices. An average yield of about 450 to 500 kg of roots and 50 kg of seed can be obtained from one acre of land.
